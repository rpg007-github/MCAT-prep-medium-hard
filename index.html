Key Pathways: The Chains You Must Know

The MCAT tests whether you can trace a signal or metabolite through an entire pathway and predict what happens when one step fails. For each pathway below, know every intermediate, the enzyme, the regulation, and what disease results when it breaks.

Glycolysis: The 10-Step Investment-Payoff

Glycolysis converts one glucose (6C) to two pyruvate (3C) in the cytoplasm. Net yield: 2 ATP (substrate-level phosphorylation) and 2 NADH. The three irreversible, regulated steps are the key to MCAT questions.

Glycolysis Regulatory Steps
Glucose → Hexokinase (or Glucokinase in liver) → G6P
 [Inhibited by G6P (hexokinase) | Glucokinase: high Km, no product inhibition]
F6P → PFK-1 (rate-limiting step) → F1,6BP
 [Activated by: AMP, F2,6BP | Inhibited by: ATP, citrate]
PEP → Pyruvate kinase → Pyruvate
 [Activated by: F1,6BP (feedforward) | Inhibited by: ATP, alanine]
Critical Misconception
PFK-1 and PFK-2 are DIFFERENT enzymes. PFK-1 is the glycolytic enzyme (F6P to F1,6BP). PFK-2 is a bifunctional enzyme (kinase/phosphatase) that makes/degrades F2,6BP, the allosteric activator of PFK-1. Insulin activates PFK-2 kinase activity (makes F2,6BP, accelerates glycolysis). Glucagon activates PFK-2 phosphatase activity (degrades F2,6BP, slows glycolysis).
MCAT Connection
Hexokinase vs. glucokinase: hexokinase is in most tissues, low Km (always active, traps glucose). Glucokinase is in liver and pancreatic beta cells, high Km (only active at high [glucose], acts as glucose sensor). Glucokinase is NOT inhibited by its product G6P.

Gluconeogenesis: The Bypass Enzymes

Gluconeogenesis produces glucose from pyruvate, primarily in the liver. It shares 7 of 10 glycolysis enzymes but replaces the 3 irreversible steps.

Gluconeogenesis Bypass Enzymes
Pyruvate → Pyruvate carboxylase (mito, biotin, activated by acetyl-CoA) → OAA
OAA → PEPCK (cytoplasm, GTP-dependent) → PEP
F1,6BP → Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase → F6P
G6P → Glucose-6-phosphatase (ER, liver/kidney ONLY) → Glucose
Systems Connection
Glucose-6-phosphatase is ONLY in liver and kidney. Muscle has no G6Pase, so muscle glycogen cannot directly raise blood glucose. Muscle sends lactate to liver (Cori cycle) or alanine (glucose-alanine cycle) for hepatic gluconeogenesis.

TCA Cycle: The Central Hub

TCA Key Regulatory Enzymes
OAA + Acetyl-CoA → Citrate synthase → Citrate [Inhibited by ATP, citrate, succinyl-CoA]
Isocitrate → Isocitrate dehydrogenase → alpha-KG + CO2 + NADH [Rate-limiting; activated by ADP, inhibited by ATP, NADH]
alpha-KG → alpha-KG dehydrogenase → Succinyl-CoA + CO2 + NADH [Requires TPP, lipoate, CoA, FAD, NAD+]
Commonly Confused
Alpha-KG dehydrogenase uses the SAME five cofactors as pyruvate dehydrogenase: TPP (B1), Lipoate, CoA (B5), FAD (B2), NAD+ (B3). Mnemonic: "Tender Loving Care For Nancy." Beriberi and Wernicke-Korsakoff (B1 deficiency) impair BOTH enzymes.

Electron Transport Chain and Poisons

ETC Complexes, Poisons, and Uncouplers
NADH → Complex I (NADH dehydrogenase) → CoQ [Poison: Rotenone, barbiturates]
FADH2 → Complex II (succinate dehydrogenase) → CoQ [Malonate inhibits; does NOT pump H+]
CoQ → Complex III (cyt bc1) → Cyt c [Poison: Antimycin A]
Cyt c → Complex IV (cyt c oxidase) → O2 → H2O [Poison: CN-, CO, H2S]
H+ gradient → ATP synthase (Complex V) → ATP [Poison: Oligomycin]

Uncouplers (DNP, thermogenin/UCP1): dissipate gradient as heat. ETC runs FASTER but NO ATP made.
Critical Distinction
ETC inhibitors (rotenone, CN-) STOP electron flow: O2 consumption DROPS, NADH accumulates. Uncouplers (DNP) INCREASE electron flow and O2 consumption but short-circuit ATP production. The MCAT specifically tests whether you confuse these two classes.
High-Yield Fact
Complex II is the ONLY complex that is both a TCA enzyme and an ETC component. It is also the ONLY complex that does NOT pump protons. FADH2 enters at Complex II, which is why FADH2 produces fewer ATP (~1.5) than NADH (~2.5).

RAAS Pathway

Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System
Low BP / Low Na+ / Sympathetic activation → JG cells → Renin
Angiotensinogen (liver) → Angiotensin I → ACE (lung) → Angiotensin II
 → Vasoconstriction (direct)
 → Aldosterone (adrenal cortex) → Na+ reabsorption, K+ secretion
 → ADH release → water reabsorption
 → Thirst → fluid intake
 → Proximal tubule Na+/H2O reabsorption
Drug Connection
ACE inhibitors block Ang II formation AND prevent bradykinin degradation. Bradykinin causes vasodilation and a dry cough (common side effect). ARBs block Ang II at its receptor but do NOT affect bradykinin, so no cough.

Insulin vs. Glucagon Signaling

Fed State: Insulin (RTK Pathway)
High glucose → Beta cells: glucokinase senses → ATP ↑ → K-ATP closes → depolarization → Ca2+ influx → insulin exocytosis
Insulin → RTK → IRS → PI3K/Akt → GLUT4 translocation, glycogen synthase ON, PFK-2 kinase ON, ACC ON (lipogenesis), K+ uptake
Fasted State: Glucagon (GPCR/Gs Pathway)
Low glucose → Alpha cells → Glucagon → GPCR (Gs) → cAMP → PKA
→ Glycogen phosphorylase ON, glycogen synthase OFF, PFK-2 phosphatase ON, HSL ON (lipolysis), PEPCK/G6Pase transcription ↑
Key Distinction
Insulin = receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK). Glucagon = GPCR coupled to Gs. Fundamentally different signaling. Insulin works through PI3K/Akt phosphorylation cascades. Glucagon works through cAMP/PKA.

Beta-Oxidation and Fatty Acid Entry

Carnitine Shuttle and Beta-Oxidation
FA + CoA → Fatty acyl-CoA (outer membrane) → CPT-I + Carnitine → Acylcarnitine
 [CPT-I inhibited by Malonyl-CoA (from ACC, fed state)]
Acylcarnitine → translocase → CPT-II → Fatty acyl-CoA (matrix)
Each cycle: 1 FADH2, 1 NADH, 1 Acetyl-CoA, chain shortened by 2C
Palmitoyl-CoA (16C): 7 cycles = 7 FADH2, 7 NADH, 8 Acetyl-CoA
Metabolic Integration
Malonyl-CoA (first committed step of FA synthesis) inhibits CPT-I. This prevents simultaneous synthesis and oxidation of fatty acids. In the fed state: insulin → ACC active → malonyl-CoA ↑ → CPT-I blocked → no beta-oxidation. In fasting: glucagon → AMPK → ACC inhibited → malonyl-CoA ↓ → CPT-I active → beta-oxidation proceeds.

Pentose Phosphate Pathway

PPP: NADPH and Ribose
G6P → G6P dehydrogenase (rate-limiting) → 6-PG + NADPH
6-PG → 6-PG dehydrogenase → Ribulose-5-P + CO2 + NADPH
Oxidative phase total: 2 NADPH per glucose
Non-oxidative phase: transketolase (TPP/B1) + transaldolase → ribose-5-P for nucleotides
G6PD Deficiency
NADPH from PPP is essential for: (1) glutathione reductase (RBC oxidative protection), (2) fatty acid synthesis, (3) CYP450 drug metabolism, (4) respiratory burst (NADPH oxidase in neutrophils). G6PD deficiency: RBCs cannot regenerate reduced glutathione. Oxidative triggers (primaquine, sulfonamides, fava beans, infections) cause Heinz bodies and hemolysis.

Urea Cycle

Urea Cycle: Mitochondria + Cytoplasm
NH4+ + CO2 + 2 ATP → CPS I (mito, requires N-acetylglutamate) → Carbamoyl-P
Carbamoyl-P + Ornithine → OTC (mito) → Citrulline [exits to cytoplasm]
Citrulline + Aspartate + ATP → ASS → Argininosuccinate
Argininosuccinate → ASL → Arginine + Fumarate [links to TCA!]
Arginine → Arginase → Urea + Ornithine (recycled)
High-Yield
OTC deficiency (X-linked, most common urea cycle defect): hyperammonemia + elevated orotic acid (excess carbamoyl-P spills into pyrimidine synthesis). The MCAT uses orotic acid to distinguish OTC deficiency from other hyperammonemia causes.

Reactions and Mechanisms You Must Master

The MCAT tests whether you can predict products by identifying functional groups and applying mechanism patterns to unfamiliar molecules. Master these core reaction types.

Amino Acid Chemistry at Physiological pH

Every amino acid question requires knowing charge states at pH 7.4. Rule: if pH > pKa, the group is deprotonated. If pH < pKa, it is protonated.

The His Trap
Histidine (pKa ~6) is the only amino acid with a side chain pKa near physiological pH. This makes it the best physiological buffer and the best catalytic residue (proton donor AND acceptor). If the MCAT asks "which amino acid at an active site," think His first.

The 2° Problem
Secondary substrates can go any of the four ways. Deciding factors: (1) strong nuc = SN2, strong base = E2, weak = SN1/E1. (2) Aprotic solvent pushes SN2/E2. (3) Heat favors elimination. The MCAT puts 2° substrates in questions specifically to test this analysis.

Carbonyl Reactivity Hierarchy

Carboxylic Acid Derivative Reactivity (Most to Least)
Acid chloride > Anhydride > Ester > Amide
Reason: Cl is least electron-donating. N in amide donates lone pair into C=O via resonance = most stable = least reactive.
You can go DOWN the ladder but NOT UP without special reagents.
Biochemistry Link
Peptide bonds are amide bonds, the LEAST reactive carboxylic acid derivative. This is why proteins are stable in water. Proteases must provide enormous rate enhancement (catalytic triad: Ser-His-Asp) to cleave them.

Misconceptions That Cost Points

Each misconception below is a specific wrong idea that MCAT answer choices are designed around. Read the wrong idea, understand why it fails, and replace it.

Enzymes Lower Ea, NOT Change Delta-G▼

Enzymes lower the activation energy (Ea). They do NOT change the free energy change (Delta-G) or the equilibrium constant (Keq). They accelerate BOTH forward and reverse reactions equally. An enzyme cannot make an endergonic reaction exergonic.

The Trap
"The enzyme makes the reaction more favorable" vs. "The enzyme makes the reaction faster." Delta-G = favorability. Ea = speed. Enzymes change speed, not favorability.
Km Is NOT Affinity (Precisely)▼

Km = substrate concentration at half-Vmax. Low Km implies high apparent affinity, but Km = (k-1 + kcat)/k1. Only when kcat is very small relative to k-1 does Km approximate Kd. Competitive inhibitors increase apparent Km (Vmax unchanged). Uncompetitive inhibitors decrease BOTH apparent Km and Vmax (students forget this).

Lineweaver-Burk Summary
Competitive: same y-intercept (Vmax), x-intercept changes (Km up). Noncompetitive: same x-intercept (Km), y-intercept changes (Vmax down). Uncompetitive: parallel lines (both change proportionally). Mixed: lines intersect in 2nd quadrant.
Delta-G vs. Delta-G-Standard Are Different▼

Delta-G-standard is at standard conditions (1M, 25C, 1 atm). Fixed property. Delta-G = actual conditions: Delta-G = Delta-G-std + RT ln(Q). A reaction with positive Delta-G-std CAN proceed if Q is small enough. Cells drive unfavorable reactions by keeping product concentrations low (coupling to ATP hydrolysis).

The Trap
You CANNOT determine reaction direction from Delta-G-std alone without knowing actual concentrations. Only Delta-G determines direction.
Codominance vs. Incomplete Dominance▼

Codominance: BOTH alleles fully expressed. Heterozygote shows BOTH phenotypes (not a blend). Example: ABO blood type (IA IB = type AB, both antigens present). Incomplete dominance: BLENDED intermediate phenotype. Example: red x white flower = pink.

Sickle Cell Subtlety
Codominant at protein level (both HbA and HbS made). Incomplete dominance at clinical level (trait carriers have mild symptoms). The MCAT can ask from either perspective.
Osmolarity vs. Tonicity▼

Osmolarity = total solute concentration. Tonicity = effect on cell volume (only counts non-penetrating solutes). A 300 mOsm urea solution is isosmotic but HYPOTONIC (urea crosses the membrane freely, does not hold water out, cell swells).

The Trap
A solution with a membrane-permeable solute at the same osmolarity as the cell appears isosmotic but is functionally hypotonic. The cell may lyse.
MHC I vs. MHC II vs. Antibodies▼

MHC I: ALL nucleated cells. Displays intracellular peptides to CD8+ T cells. MHC II: APCs only (dendritic, macrophages, B cells). Displays exogenous peptides to CD4+ T cells. Antibodies: recognize native (3D) antigens directly. T cells ONLY see linear peptides on MHC.

The Trap
T cells CANNOT directly recognize free-floating antigens. They require antigen presentation on MHC. B cells/antibodies CAN bind free antigens.
Action Potential: Closed vs. Inactivated Na+ Channels▼

Closed channel: can be opened by depolarization. Inactivated channel: CANNOT reopen until it resets (ball-and-chain blocks pore). Cycle: Closed (resting) → Open (depolarizing) → Inactivated (ball blocks) → Closed (repolarization resets). Absolute refractory period = inactivation, not closure.

Le Chatelier: Only Temperature Changes K▼

Adding/removing reactants, changing pressure, adding catalysts: shift equilibrium position but do NOT change K. Temperature is the ONLY factor that changes K. Exothermic: heat increases, K decreases (shifts left). Endothermic: heat increases, K increases (shifts right).

Hardy-Weinberg: Five Assumptions, One Equation▼

p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1. Five conditions for equilibrium: no mutation, no selection, no migration, no drift (infinite pop), random mating. Most common MCAT format: given disease frequency (q2), find carrier frequency (2pq). Remember carrier = 2pq, not just pq.

Parasympathetic: Both Pre/Post Use ACh But Different Receptors▼

ALL preganglionic neurons (both divisions): ACh at nicotinic receptors (ion channels, fast). Parasympathetic postganglionic: ACh at muscarinic (GPCRs, slow). Sympathetic postganglionic: NE at adrenergic (except sweat glands = ACh muscarinic). Atropine (muscarinic blocker) blocks parasympathetic postganglionic effects ONLY, not ganglionic nicotinic transmission.

Multi-System Integration: Tracing Cascades

The MCAT's hardest questions present a clinical scenario and ask about downstream effects in a completely different organ system. Know the chains.

DKA Cascade (Type 1 Diabetes)

Complete DKA Chain
No insulin → Cells starve (can't uptake glucose)
→ Liver: gluconeogenesis + ketogenesis (unregulated lipolysis → FFA → ketones)
→ Blood: hyperglycemia + ketonemia → metabolic acidosis
→ Kidney: glucose exceeds threshold (180 mg/dL) → glycosuria → osmotic diuresis
 → Polyuria → dehydration → polydipsia
→ Electrolytes: H+ enters cells, K+ exits + no insulin to drive K+ in → serum K+ HIGH (despite total body K+ depletion)
→ Respiratory: Kussmaul breathing (compensatory for acidosis)
→ TREATMENT DANGER: insulin → K+ shifts in → hypokalemia → arrhythmia risk

CKD Multi-System Failure

Chronic Kidney Disease Cascade
Nephron loss →
1. Fluid/Na+: retention → HTN → LVH → heart failure
2. EPO: decreased → normocytic anemia → fatigue
3. Vitamin D: 1-alpha-hydroxylase loss → no calcitriol → low Ca2+ absorption
 → Secondary hyperparathyroidism → bone resorption → renal osteodystrophy
4. Acid-base: can't excrete H+ → metabolic acidosis
5. K+: can't excrete → hyperkalemia → arrhythmia risk
6. Phosphate: can't excrete → hyperphosphatemia → binds Ca2+ → worsens hypocalcemia
MCAT Application
If a passage describes fatigue, bone pain, low Hb, low Ca2+, high PTH, high phosphate, low GFR: "Which single organ's dysfunction explains ALL findings?" Answer: kidney.

Heart Failure: Forward and Backward

Left Heart Failure
BACKWARD: blood backs into pulmonary veins → pulmonary edema → dyspnea
FORWARD: decreased CO → decreased renal perfusion → RAAS activation → Na+/H2O retention → MORE volume → WORSE failure
Most common cause of RIGHT heart failure = LEFT heart failure (pulmonary congestion → pulmonary HTN → RV overload)
Drug Targets
ACE inhibitors: break RAAS cycle. Loop diuretics: reduce volume directly. Beta-blockers: reduce HR and remodeling. The MCAT may describe each by mechanism and ask WHERE in the chain it acts.

O2 Dissociation Curve: Shifts and Traps

Right Shift = Release O2 to Tissues
RIGHT: increased temp, H+ (Bohr), CO2, 2,3-BPG. Tissues that need O2 are hot, acidic, high CO2.
LEFT: decreased temp, H+, CO2, 2,3-BPG. Also: CO, fetal Hb (HbF), methemoglobin.
CO: 200x affinity. Left-shifts remaining Hb. Pulse ox can't distinguish HbCO from HbO2.
HbF: gamma subunits bind 2,3-BPG poorly → higher O2 affinity → pulls O2 from maternal blood.

MCAT Advanced Strategy

Passage Deconstruction

Step 1: Read the LAST sentence first (main conclusion). Step 2: Identify IV, DV, controls. Step 3: Scan figures/tables before passage text. Step 4: Read passage for context. Spend no more than 3 min reading, 1.5 min per question.

Answer Elimination Hierarchy

Step 1: Eliminate factually incorrect answers. Step 2: Eliminate true but irrelevant answers. Step 3: Eliminate true/relevant but incomplete answers. Step 4: Choose the most direct, complete explanation.

"True But Not The Answer" Trap
A question about WHY a diabetic has bone loss. "The patient has elevated glucose" is true but does not explain bone loss. "Impaired renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase reduces calcium absorption" is true AND explains the mechanism. Always ask: does this answer the SPECIFIC question?
